# ArdaNova linguistic guide

## Purpose

ArdaNova is a social layer for collective action: **social media for doing, not doom-scrolling**. It uses technology to make contribution, decision-making, and explicitly documented rights more legible - **empowering ownership, not surveillance**.

This guide turns that position into repeatable language for product interfaces, community activity, Nova-assisted work, documentation, and public storytelling. It complements the [brand guidelines](./BRAND_GUIDELINES.md) and the [frontend experience contract](./FRONTEND_EXPERIENCE.md).

The linguistic north star is:

> **Turn attention into accountable action.**

---

## The solutionary position

**Solutionary** is a deliberate ArdaNova word. It describes a person, project, or practice that moves from a lived problem to a testable response and keeps learning in public with the people affected.

A solutionary approach is revolutionary because it replaces passive reaction, spectacle, and savior narratives with shared inquiry and sustained work. Use the word when that full meaning is present; do not use it as a decorative synonym for "innovative."

Good uses:

- "Work the solutionary loop."
- "A solutionary project begins with the people who experience the problem."
- "Solutionary is revolutionary."

Avoid:

- "Meet the world's leading solutionaries" when there is no evidence of their work.
- "Our solutionary technology fixes everything" because tools do not own the outcome.
- Repeating "revolutionary" around routine features or product announcements.

Use **solutionary** in normal sentence case. Reserve manifesto language for a few high-signal moments; the working interface should remain concrete.

## The core loop

ArdaNova organizes attention around one recurring loop:

```text
Discover a problem -> Define a solution -> Iterate
        ^                                      |
        +--------------------------------------+
```

This is a loop, not a funnel. Iteration may reveal that the problem was framed incorrectly, that different people need to be involved, or that a project should stop. Returning to discovery is progress when the evidence calls for it.

### 1. Discover a problem

Begin with lived experience, affected people, observable conditions, and unanswered questions. Discovery gathers context; it does not manufacture certainty or mine attention for trends.

Use:

- discover, notice, document, listen, map, ask, surface
- problem statement, field note, evidence, affected people, open question, constraint
- "What is happening?"
- "Who experiences it, and how?"
- "What do we know, and what remains uncertain?"

Avoid:

- pain point when it reduces a human problem to a sales opportunity
- trending problem, viral issue, or hot topic as a proxy for importance
- target audience when the people affected are participants in the work

The useful outcome of discovery is a problem that people can inspect, challenge, and decide to work on.

### 2. Define a solution

Turn context into a proposal with visible assumptions, scope, roles, terms, and ways to judge progress. A solution is a hypothesis for coordinated action, not a promise of certainty.

Use:

- define, shape, propose, scope, compare, set terms, invite, commit
- solution brief, hypothesis, milestone, role, responsibility, participation terms, success measure
- "What change are we proposing?"
- "What is in scope, and what is not?"
- "Who decides, who contributes, and under what terms?"

Avoid:

- perfect solution, guaranteed outcome, instant fix
- ownership as shorthand for membership, project-token utility, or future economic value
- community-owned unless the governing documents and applicable rights support the claim

The useful outcome of definition is a reviewable proposal. Any ownership, governance, compensation, transfer, or redemption right must be named and documented separately.

### 3. Iterate

Put the proposal into practice, compare what happened with what was intended, and revise with the people affected. Iteration values evidence and repair over performance theater.

Use:

- test, contribute, build, compare, learn, revise, reconcile, continue, pause, stop
- iteration, work update, decision, result, exception, lesson, next move
- "What changed?"
- "What did we learn?"
- "What should the next version keep, change, or stop?"

Avoid:

- ship it as a substitute for review
- done when settlement, rights, or downstream effects remain unresolved
- growth, momentum, or engagement without naming the meaningful activity measured

The useful outcome of iteration is evidence for the next decision. It may lead forward, back to discovery, or to a documented stop.

## Social language: activity, not attention

ArdaNova is social because people discover, decide, and build together. It is not a generic attention feed. Product language should direct people toward meaningful objects and finite next moves.

### Preferred community vocabulary

| Prefer | Use it for |
| --- | --- |
| Workstream | An ordered view of project activity and next moves |
| Field note | Firsthand context or evidence from discovery |
| Open question | Something the group needs to understand or decide |
| Contribution | Work, knowledge, resources, or support offered under visible terms |
| Work update | What changed, what was learned, and what comes next |
| Proposal | A reviewable suggestion that has not yet become a decision |
| Decision | A recorded outcome made under stated authority |
| Iteration | A bounded cycle of action, learning, and revision |
| Invitation | A specific request for a person, skill, perspective, or resource |
| Support | A named action, not a vague popularity signal |
| Track | Follow a project because its work matters to you |

Use **post** only when the object is truly general communication. Prefer the more exact noun - field note, question, proposal, update, decision, or invitation - whenever possible.

### Avoid engagement-extraction language

Do not use these as product goals or celebratory metrics:

- maximize engagement, time on platform, stickiness, impressions, or content velocity
- go viral, boost reach, trending now, most addictive, never miss out
- users as an audience to capture, monetize, or convert
- infinite feed, endless discovery, or "keep scrolling"
- popularity presented as proof that a problem or solution matters

If a metric is useful, name the work it represents: open questions answered, contributors matched, proposals reviewed, milestones reconciled, or iterations completed.

### Activity sentence pattern

Write community activity as:

```text
Person + meaningful verb + project object + useful context
```

Examples:

- "Mara added field evidence to Water Commons."
- "The repair team revised the second milestone after community review."
- "Three contributors offered translation support under the posted terms."

Avoid vague activity such as "Mara engaged with Water Commons" or "This project is trending."

## Ownership without surveillance

Technology should make power legible, not make people more observable for its own sake.

### Ownership language

The word **ownership** must always have an object and a basis. Say what is owned, what rights attach to it, where those rights are documented, and what state the record has reached.

Use:

- "You control this draft."
- "The project terms offer a separately documented ownership share after approval and reconciliation."
- "This membership credential grants the stated participation rights; it does not represent equity."
- "This project token has only the utility described in the project terms."

Avoid:

- "You own the project" after saving, joining, contributing, or receiving a token
- "Earn ownership" without naming the instrument, conditions, and reconciliation state
- treating credentials, tokens, shares, governance authority, and compensation as interchangeable

"Own what you build" is a brand aspiration. In transactional product copy, replace aspiration with the exact documented right.

### Anti-surveillance language

Interfaces should name what context is used, why it is needed, what is excluded, and who can act on it. Do not imply background profiling or celebrate behavioral extraction.

Prefer:

- context you selected, sources in scope, permission, consent, visibility, review, control
- "Nova used the project brief and the three notes you selected."
- "Your location helps match this task to a service area" only when true and necessary
- a plain explanation before requesting identity, wallet, location, or financial information

Avoid:

- "We know what you need" or "The platform understands you"
- "We track everything so your experience gets better"
- hyper-personalized, behavioral signals, data exhaust, audience intelligence
- hidden-inference language such as "recommended for you" when the basis cannot be explained

Do not invent privacy promises. Claims such as "we never track," "your data is yours," retention periods, or deletion guarantees must match implemented behavior and the governing policy.

## Voice and tone

ArdaNova sounds:

- **Concrete:** name the artifact, action, person, and state.
- **Energized:** use strong verbs and forward movement without hype.
- **Collective:** write with people, not at an abstract "community."
- **Candid:** state uncertainty, exclusions, and prerequisites early.
- **Invitational:** offer a meaningful next move instead of manufacturing urgency.
- **Accountable:** identify who can decide and what evidence supports the state.

ArdaNova does not sound:

- corporate, extractive, paternalistic, techno-mystical, or triumphalist
- like a savior arriving to "give people a voice"
- like a casino, speculative market, growth hack, or productivity surveillance tool
- falsely casual around rights, identity, governance, money, or settlement

Prefer **people**, **participants**, **contributors**, **project stewards**, **supporters**, and **members** when those roles are accurate. Use **user** in technical documentation or when no more meaningful role is known.

## Sentence patterns

### Position -> mechanism -> limit

> "Turn attention into action. Work through a shared project brief, visible decisions, and bounded iterations. Nothing publishes or changes rights without explicit review."

### State -> evidence -> next move

> "Release instruction submitted. The receiving service returned reference 41A7. Check confirmation before treating the settlement as reconciled."

### Invitation -> contribution -> purpose

> "Add a field note about transit access so the team can test the service-area assumption."

### Observation -> proposal -> question

> "Three interviews describe the same repair delay. Nova proposes a shared intake milestone. Should the project test it with one neighborhood first?"

Sentence case is the default. Use short uppercase labels only for navigation, metadata, or an intentional display moment.

## Calls to action

A CTA names the immediate action and, when useful, its object. It should not imply a later state that the action cannot complete.

### Discover

- Describe the problem
- Add field evidence
- Invite an affected participant
- Review open questions

### Define

- Shape a solution
- Name the terms
- Compare proposals
- Review the solution brief
- Draft with Nova

### Iterate

- Record what changed
- Review this iteration
- Reconcile the result
- Start the next iteration
- Return to the problem

Avoid vague or inflated CTAs such as **Engage**, **Boost**, **Go viral**, **Let AI solve it**, **Claim ownership**, or **Complete settlement** when the action only submits an instruction.

## Nova and machine language

Nova works beside an artifact. It may ask, draft, organize, compare, review, present, and rehearse. It does not know, decide, approve, own, publish, fund, settle, or grant rights.

Prefer:

- "Nova proposes..."
- "Drafted from the selected project brief and two work updates."
- "This suggestion assumes..."
- "Review the three changes before accepting them."
- "No funding or rights-changing action is included."

Avoid:

- "Nova solved your project."
- "AI-powered magic"
- "One-click project creation" when review is required
- human-like certainty, emotion, or authority the system cannot substantiate
- presenting generated copy as a decision, publication, or completed external action

Nova messages should expose scope, exclusions, assumptions, uncertainty, proposed changes, and the exact effect of acceptance. The human controls are **Accept**, **Edit**, **Reject**, and **Undo**.

## Reusable brand lines

Use these selectively; they are anchors, not a bag of slogans.

### Primary

- Social media for doing, not doom-scrolling.
- Technology for ownership, not surveillance.
- Discover a problem. Define a solution. Iterate.
- Solutionary is revolutionary.

### Supporting

- Turn attention into action.
- Make the next iteration visible.
- Build the work. Make the deal visible.
- Progress is something people can inspect.

Any line about ownership must sit near copy that explains explicit rights. Any line about privacy or surveillance must remain consistent with implemented behavior and policy.

## Editorial check

Before shipping product or campaign copy, ask:

1. Does this direct attention toward a meaningful action rather than more consumption?
2. Is the current stage - discover, define, or iterate - clear?
3. Are people active subjects rather than targets, audiences, or data sources?
4. Is every ownership or governance claim tied to explicit terms?
5. Does the copy distinguish draft, submitted, confirmed, and reconciled states?
6. Does Nova's language disclose scope, uncertainty, and human control?
7. Is the next move concrete, finite, and honest?

If any answer is no, revise before release.
